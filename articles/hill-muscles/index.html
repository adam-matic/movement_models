Hill-type muscles

Muscles produce force by converting chemical energy into mechanical work. The Hill model — originally proposed by A.V. Hill in 1938 and refined for simulations by Thelen (2003) — describes how the force a muscle can produce depends on three things: how long the muscle is, how fast it is changing length, and how activated it is.

Force-length relationship

A muscle has an optimal length at which it produces peak force. Stretch it too far or compress it too much, and force drops. There are two components: the active force (from cross-bridge cycling, driven by activation) and the passive force (from elastic structures like titin, present even without activation).

Active (blue), passive (orange), and total (dark) force vs. normalized muscle length. Dashed line shows force at current length.

Force-velocity relationship

Force also depends on the rate of length change. During shortening (concentric contraction), force decreases — the faster the muscle shortens, the less force it can produce. During lengthening (eccentric contraction), force rises above isometric. The Hill hyperbola captures the concentric side; a linear extension handles the eccentric side.

Normalized force vs. normalized velocity. Negative velocity = shortening; positive = lengthening.

Activation dynamics

Neural signals don’t directly set muscle force — they drive activation through a first-order filter. Activation rises with time constant τact = 75 ms and decays more slowly with τdeact = 50 ms. This filtering limits how fast you can switch muscles on and off.

Putting it together

The total muscle force is:

F = F₀ · [ a · fL(l) · fV(v) + fPE(l) ]

where F₀ is the maximum isometric force, a is activation (0–1), fL is the active force-length curve, fV is the force-velocity curve, and fPE is the passive force-length curve.

This model, combined with a skeleton and a controller, forms the basis for the simulations in the articles that follow.
