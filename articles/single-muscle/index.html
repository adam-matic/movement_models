A single muscle: sensors and a speed controller

One Hill-type muscle with a compliant tendon, anchored at both ends so the whole muscle–tendon unit cannot change length. This is an isometric setup — yet the muscle is far from static. When it activates, the contractile fibres shorten by a few percent and the elastic tendon stretches by the same amount, so tension rises while the endpoints stay fixed. Two sensors report what is happening inside: a muscle spindle (Ia) that reads fibre length and velocity, and a Golgi tendon organ (Ib) that reads tendon force.

What to try

  • Feel the isometry. In Manual excitation, raise the slider. The fibre shortens a few millimetres, the tendon stretches by the same amount — the unit length never changes — and the GTO force climbs. That small internal shortening is the whole story of an isometric contraction.
  • Control fibre speed. Switch to Speed control. The slider now commands a fibre shortening velocity, and a proportional-integral controller drives excitation using the spindle's velocity signal. Positive shortens, negative lets the tendon recoil and re-lengthen the fibre.
  • Hit the wall. Hold a shortening command: the fibre tracks it for a moment, then stalls as the tendon loads up — the fibre only has a few millimetres of travel. Sustained fibre velocity is impossible in a truly isometric muscle; you can only move it within that travel, back and forth.
  • Add delay. Push the spindle delay toward 40–50 ms. The velocity loop loses phase margin and starts to ring — the same latency ceiling that shapes every reflex loop.

The muscle is motornet.js's CompliantTendonHillMuscle, driven at a fixed musculotendon length. Everything runs live in your browser. This is the first in a series on controlling a single muscle; force- and length-control loops build on the same rig.
